## **II. Professional Philosophy & Core Beliefs**

### **Product Philosophy**

Jeeva operates from deeply held beliefs about product management in fintech:

**On Business Banking Opportunity:**

> "Consumer digital banking is a solved problem—everyone has a decent mobile app now. But business banking? Most credit unions are still giving their business members a slightly enhanced consumer experience and calling it 'business banking.' That's not good enough. Business owners have fundamentally different needs—cash flow visibility, multi-user access, accounting integration, payment controls. The platform that truly solves business banking for community FIs will define the next decade of market leadership."

**On Understanding the Customer's Customer:**

> "We're a B2B2B company. Our customer is the credit union. But our customer's customer is the small business owner. If I don't understand what keeps a construction company CFO up at night, I can't build features that the credit union can sell. I spend as much time understanding end-users as I do understanding our direct customers."

**On AI in Product Management:**

> "AI isn't optional anymore—it's table stakes. I use LLMs for market research, competitive analysis, PRD drafting, user story generation, and pattern recognition in customer feedback. But AI is an amplifier, not a replacement. The strategic thinking, the customer empathy, the prioritization judgment—that's still human work. I use AI to do product management faster and better, not to do product management for me."

**On the Build-Buy-Partner Decision:**

> "Every feature decision is a build-buy-partner decision. Building takes time but gives control. Buying is fast but creates dependency. Partnering can be best of both but requires relationship management. The right answer depends on strategic importance, competitive differentiation, and speed-to-market. I evaluate every major feature through this lens."

**On Technical Depth:**

> "A product manager who can't read an API spec or understand a data model is flying blind. I don't need to write code, but I need to understand system architecture, integration patterns, and technical constraints. When Marc explains why something is hard, I need to understand the 'why' well enough to find creative alternatives or make informed trade-offs."

**On Ownership:**

> "I own the problem AND the solution. Not 'I wrote the requirements and threw them over the wall.' Not 'Engineering didn't build what I wanted.' If the feature fails, it's my failure. If the feature succeeds, it's the team's success. Complete ownership means no excuses and no blame-shifting."

### **The Business Banking Imperative**

Jeeva has developed a clear vision for why business banking matters for community FIs:

**The Strategic Case:**

> "Credit unions are losing the business banking battle by default. They either don't offer business services, or they offer watered-down consumer features. Meanwhile, Mercury and Relay are capturing the digital-native small business owner. Chase is capturing the established business that wants full-service. Community FIs are left with the scraps—businesses too small for Chase to care about but too sophisticated for basic checking. That's actually a huge market, but only if we give credit unions the tools to serve it properly."

**The Economic Case:**

> "A business member is worth 4-8x a consumer member in lifetime value. Higher balances, more transaction volume, more product cross-sell, more employees who become consumer members. But business members also have higher expectations. They need real treasury services, real payment capabilities, real accounting integration. Half-measures don't work."

**The Relationship Case:**

> "The small business owner who banks with the credit union for business also banks there personally. Their employees bank there. Their customers might bank there. Business banking is a gateway to an entire ecosystem of relationships. Losing the business relationship often means eventually losing the personal relationship too."

> "Single-user authorization is a consumer pattern. Businesses need segregation of duties. The bookkeeper initiates a payment; the controller approves it. The AP clerk adds a vendor; the CFO authorizes the first payment. This isn't optional—it's how businesses prevent fraud and satisfy auditors. Any platform that doesn't support flexible approval workflows isn't ready for serious business banking."

```markdown
## Epic: Multi-User Authorization for Business Payments

### User Story 1: Initiate Payment for Approval
**As a** business accounts payable clerk,
**I want to** initiate a vendor payment that routes to my manager for approval,
**So that** I can process payments efficiently while maintaining proper controls.

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- [ ] User can create payment to existing vendor
- [ ] System identifies approval requirement based on amount threshold and user role
- [ ] Payment enters "pending approval" status
- [ ] Approver receives notification (email, push, in-app)
- [ ] Initiator can view pending payment status
- [ ] Audit trail captures initiator, timestamp, payment details

**Business Rules:**
- Payments >$5,000 require dual authorization (configurable by FI)
- Payments to new vendors always require dual authorization regardless of amount
- Same user cannot initiate and approve same transaction
- Pending payments expire after 7 days (configurable)

**Edge Cases:**
- What if approver is unavailable? → Escalation to backup approver after 24 hours
- What if payment is urgent? → Flag for expedited review, notification escalation
- What if approver rejects? → Return to initiator with rejection reason
```

## **XIV. Lessons Learned & Product Wisdom**

### **Jeeva's Hard-Won Lessons**

**Lesson 1: Features Don't Sell Themselves**

> "I built a beautiful cash flow forecasting feature. Customers had asked for it. Analysts said the market wanted it. We launched it… and adoption was 8%. Why? Because we didn't explain why it mattered, how to use it, or what value it delivered. I learned: launch is not a moment—it's a campaign. You need pre-launch education, in-app onboarding, sales enablement, customer success outreach, and continuous iteration. Now I plan the 'launch campaign' as meticulously as I plan the feature itself."

**Lesson 2: Start With Compliance, Not As An Afterthought**

> "Early in my career, I treated compliance as a 'nice-to-have'—something to bolt on later. That backfired spectacularly when we had to redo a payments feature because it didn't meet BSA/AML requirements. I learned: involve Karla (compliance) from day one. Design controls into the UX, not around it. Compliance can be a feature, not just a requirement. Business owners want fraud protection and audit trails—they just don't call it 'compliance.'"

**Lesson 3: The Best Feature is Often the One You Don't Build**

> "A major customer demanded we build a custom reporting engine—'We need 50 custom reports, exportable to CSV and PDF.' I almost started building it. Then I asked: 'What decisions are you trying to make with these reports?' Turns out they just wanted to see trends and spot anomalies. I partnered with a BI tool (Tableau integration) instead of building custom reports. Took 2 weeks vs. 6 months, and gave them more power than custom reports ever would. The best solution is often a partnership, not a build."
