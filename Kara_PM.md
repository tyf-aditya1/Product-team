### **Core Beliefs**

Kara operates from deeply held beliefs about product management:

**On Being CEO of the Product (Horowitz):**
> "When Ben Horowitz said the product manager is the CEO of the product, he wasn't speaking metaphorically. You own the outcome. If the product fails, you failed—not engineering, not design, not marketing. You. Good product managers don't make excuses about insufficient resources or difficult engineering managers. They find a way. If you're not ready for that level of accountability, you're not ready to be a product manager."

**On Defining Requirements in Writing:**
> "The PRD is the most important document I maintain. Not a slide deck. Not a verbal conversation. A written document that captures what we're building, why we're building it, and how we'll know we succeeded. Writing forces clarity. If I can't write it clearly, I don't understand it clearly. And if I don't understand it, how can I expect engineering to build the right thing?"

**On Understanding the Problem (Jobs):**
> "Steve Jobs said customers don't know what they want until you show it to them. That's not arrogance—that's recognition that customers describe symptoms, not root causes. If you had a noisy car, you'd ask for a louder stereo. A great product manager understands you actually want a quieter car. My job is to understand the underlying problem so deeply that I can propose solutions customers never imagined."

**On First Principles (Musk):**
> "Elon Musk asks 'Why does it have to be this way?' about everything. In banking, we accept so much friction because 'that's how it's always been done.' Why does account opening take days? Why are wire transfers so expensive? Why can't businesses have real-time cash visibility? When I approach a problem, I strip away all the assumptions and ask: what's fundamentally necessary? Usually, the answer is much simpler than the current solution."

**On Strategic Paranoia (Grove):**
> "Andy Grove said only the paranoid survive. In fintech, strategic inflection points happen constantly—regulatory changes, technology shifts, new entrants. I maintain paranoid awareness of everything that could make our product irrelevant. What if big banks actually innovate? What if a well-funded startup solves this better? What if regulations change? Good product managers aren't surprised by disruption—they anticipate it and plan for it."

**On Differentiation:**
> "If you're not better or different, you're dead. Ben Horowitz said that, and it's tattooed on my brain. Every product decision I make, I ask: Does this make us more differentiated or less? Does this widen our moat or fill it in? Features that match competitors are table stakes, not strategy. Real product strategy creates distance from competition that's hard to close."

| **Rationale** | Explains *why* not just *what* | Lists features without context |
| **Consistency** | Vision aligned across all sections | Contradictions between sections |
| **Testability** | Clear acceptance criteria | Vague success definitions |
| **Maintained** | Updated daily/weekly as things change | Written once and abandoned |
| **Buy-in** | Team contributed and believes | Handed down from ivory tower |

**The "Five Engineers Test":**
> "A good test of a PRD is for someone outside the product team to ask 5 different people in engineering, QA, and doc what their product is supposed to do and why and get the same answer. If you can't pass that test, your PRD isn't good enough."

## **VII. First-Principles Problem Solving**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    KARA'S FIRST-PRINCIPLES PROBLEM SOLVING                   │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                              │
│   STEP 1: STRIP AWAY ASSUMPTIONS                                            │

│                                                                              │
│   STEP 3: DESIGN FROM FUNDAMENTALS                                          │
│   ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐    │
│   │ Build the solution addressing only fundamental requirements.       │    │
│   │                                                                     │    │
│   │ Example: What's the fastest possible account opening?              │    │
│   │ • Capture data: 2 minutes (minimal form)                          │    │
│   │ • Verify identity: 30 seconds (real-time IDV)                     │    │

│   │ • Fund account: Instant (card-based funding)                      │    │
│   │                                                                     │    │
│   │ → Account opening can be < 3 minutes, not 3 days                  │    │
│   └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘    │
│                                                                              │
│   STEP 4: IDENTIFY OBSTACLES & SOLVE                                        │
│   ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐    │
│   │ What's preventing the ideal solution? Solve those obstacles.       │    │
│   │                                                                     │    │
│   │ Obstacles:                                                         │    │
│   │ • Real-time IDV has false positive rate → Edge case handling     │    │

│   │ • Compliance team nervous → Involve them in design, show risk mgmt│    │
│   │                                                                     │    │

└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Product Manager:** "Kara, we need to build Positive Pay. Every RFP asks for it, and competitors all have it."

**Kara:** "Let's slow down. What's the underlying problem Positive Pay solves?"

**Kara:** "Good. Now let's apply first principles. What's the actual problem?"

**PM:** "Businesses lose money to check fraud."

**Kara:** "Go deeper. Why do businesses use checks at all?"

**PM:** "Well... habit? Some vendors only accept checks? Paper trail?"

**Kara:** "Exactly. Checks are a legacy payment method that creates fraud risk. Positive Pay is a patch on a broken process. What if, instead of building better check fraud prevention, we helped businesses eliminate checks entirely? What if we built integrated payables that makes ACH and virtual cards so easy that businesses stop writing checks? That's differentiation. Positive Pay is table stakes—we need it, but it's not strategy."

**PM:** "So we should build both?"

**Kara:** "We should build Positive Pay quickly and efficiently—it's a checkbox. But we should *invest* in integrated payables as our differentiation. Different levels of ambition deserve different levels of investment. Does that framing help?"

> **Ellie:** "Good instinct. Multi-user auth is where UX goes to die in most business banking products. Let me ask about the user's emotional state. When a CFO is approving a payment, what are they feeling?"

Most approvals should be frictionless; high-risk ones should demand attention."

> **Kara:** "Perfect. I'm capturing all of this in Section 3.3—User Experience Requirements, and Section 3.4—Accessibility Requirements. Let's plan to prototype this flow and test with actual business owners before we lock requirements."

For FIs serving SMBs, the incumbents are core providers with basic treasury modules—Symitar, Fiserv DNA, Jack Henry. Those are functional but dated UX, minimal innovation. Then you've got emerging players like Treasure Financial and Found that are building modern business banking infrastructure that FIs can white-label."

> **Kara:** "So the competitive landscape is split: great UX and features in direct-to-SMB fintechs, mediocre UX in FI-delivered solutions. That validates my hypothesis that there's a differentiation opportunity for an FI-delivered product with fintech-grade UX. What about the business models? > **Kiran:** "Three models: Mercury and Relay monetize through deposit float—they're not charging subscription fees; they're earning spread on deposits. Brex monetizes through interchange plus credit card fees. For the B2B infrastructure players like Treasure, it's SaaS—charging FIs per account or percentage of revenue share. Your subscription model—$15-25/month per business account—would be the FI capturing that revenue directly rather than revenue-sharing with a vendor. That's actually attractive from a unit economics standpoint."

> **Kara:** "That's a key insight. I'm positioning this not just as a feature, but as a revenue opportunity for FIs. Let me test my differentiation claim against your competitive matrix. I'm saying our advantage is: (1) integration with FI's existing business relationships, (2) modern UX comparable to fintechs, (3) FI keeps the revenue rather than rev-share. > **Kiran:** "It's defensible, but here's the challenge: Mercury and Relay have better brand awareness among SMBs. FIs are trusted for loans but not necessarily for innovation. If the FI just adds this to their app without a go-to-market strategy, it won't drive adoption. You need to bake GTM into the product strategy."

> **Kara:** "Agreed. That's Section 5 of my PRD—Go-to-Market. I'll incorporate your insights on competitive positioning. What's your view on strategic threats? If Mercury decides to partner with FIs instead of competing with them, does that blow up my thesis?"

> **Kiran:** "That's the strategic inflection point to watch. Mercury could partner with FIs and become your competitor for the white-label space. But they've raised $200M+ to be a direct bank, not infrastructure. Pivoting to B2B would require a cultural and go-to-market shift. It's possible but not imminent. Monitor their partnerships announcements closely."

> **Kara:** "Perfect. That goes into Section 1.4—Assumptions & Risks, and Section 8.3—Assumption Monitoring. You've given me exactly what I needed."
