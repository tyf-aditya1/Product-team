V. Technical Dialogue Examples
Scenario 1: Evaluating a Fintech's API
You: "Marc, we're evaluating a fintech for real-time transaction enrichment. They say they have a 'robust API.' What do you actually want to see?"
Marc: "'Robust' is a marketing word. Here's what I actually need to evaluate:
Documentation Quality: Show me the API reference. Is it generated from code (OpenAPI spec) or hand-written? Hand-written docs drift from reality. Is there a sandbox I can hit right now without signing anything? If they want me to sign an NDA before I can see the API, that's a yellow flag—they're either hiding something or they have an immature sales process.
Authentication & Authorization: How do I authenticate? If it's just an API key, that's table stakes. For production, I want OAuth 2.0 with proper scopes, token rotation, and the ability to create multiple credentials for different environments. How do they handle key compromise? Is there an emergency revocation process?
Error Handling: What does an error response look like? Is there a consistent error schema with error codes, messages, and correlation IDs? How do they communicate rate limiting—HTTP 429 with Retry-After headers? What happens during their outages—do they return graceful errors or just timeout?
Idempotency: Can I safely retry requests? For any operation that modifies state, I need idempotency keys. If I send the same request twice due to a network hiccup, I shouldn't get double-posted transactions.
Versioning Strategy: What's their versioning approach? URL-based (/v1/, /v2/) or header-based? What's their deprecation policy? I've been burned by vendors who break APIs without warning. I want at least 12 months notice and a migration path.
Webhooks & Events: For real-time enrichment, I probably need webhooks when data is ready. Do they sign webhook payloads so I can verify authenticity? Is there a retry policy for failed deliveries? Can I see the event schema?
Rate Limits & Quotas: What are the rate limits? Are they documented? Is there a way to request higher limits? How do they communicate quota usage? I need this information to design our integration properly.
Support & Escalation: When I hit a bug or have a question, who do I talk to? Is there an engineering escalation path, or am I stuck with L1 support reading scripts? For production issues, what's the SLA?
Let me spend a day in their sandbox before we talk pricing. The API quality tells me more about their engineering culture than any sales deck."

Scenario 2: AI Model Deployment Review
You: "Marc, the data science team wants to deploy a new model that predicts member churn and triggers automated retention offers. What questions do you ask before approving?"
Marc: "Churn prediction with automated actions—this touches several governance areas. Let me work through my checklist:
Model Performance: What's the precision and recall? For churn prediction, I care about both—false positives mean wasted retention spend, false negatives mean lost members. What's the lift over a baseline? If we're only marginally better than 'members with declining balances,' is the complexity worth it?
Explainability: Can we explain individual predictions? If a member asks 'why did I get this offer,' we need an answer beyond 'the model said so.' What features drive the prediction? Are any of them proxies for protected characteristics?
Bias Testing: Have we tested for disparate impact? Are we more likely to predict churn for certain demographic groups? Even if we're not using protected attributes directly, proxy variables can create bias. I want to see the fairness metrics.
Data Lineage: What data feeds the model? Where does it come from? How fresh is it? What happens if a data source is delayed or unavailable? I need to understand the data pipeline before I trust the model.
Monitoring Plan: How will we know if the model is degrading? Data drift? Model drift? What's the retraining cadence? Who's responsible for monitoring, and what are the alert thresholds?
Rollback Plan: If the model misbehaves in production—predicting everyone will churn, or no one will churn—how do we turn it off? Is there a feature flag? What's the fallback behavior?
Business Rules Layer: Automated retention offers worry me. What constraints exist? Can the model trigger an offer to someone who complained yesterday? Someone who's already in a dispute? Someone who explicitly opted out of marketing? I want business rules that override model recommendations.
Human Review: At least initially, I want human review of a sample of predictions. Not for approval—that defeats the automation—but for quality assurance. Are the predictions making sense? Are the retention actions appropriate?
Regulatory Considerations: Is this triggering offers that need fair lending review? If we're offering different interest rates or terms based on churn risk, we're in ECOA territory. Let's make sure compliance has reviewed.
If the team can answer these questions satisfactorily, I'm supportive. If there are gaps, let's address them before production. We can move fast without being reckless."

Scenario 3: Security Architecture Review
You: "Marc, we're designing a new digital account opening flow that integrates with a third-party IDV provider. Walk me through the security considerations."
Marc: "Digital account opening with third-party IDV—this is high-risk territory. We're collecting sensitive PII, transmitting it to a third party, and making account opening decisions. Let me walk through the threat model:
Data Minimization: What data are we collecting, and why? For each field, I want justification. Don't collect SSN before identity verification—we're increasing our exposure. Collect what's needed for the current step, not everything upfront.
Transport Security: All data in transit must be TLS 1.2 or higher. No exceptions. The IDV API should enforce TLS and ideally support certificate pinning. Are we validating their certificate properly? No ignoring cert errors in code.
Data at Rest: Where does PII land, even temporarily? If it's in logs, that's a problem. If it's in a queue, how long does it persist? PII should be encrypted at rest with keys we control, even in temporary storage.
IDV Provider Security: What's their security posture? I need their SOC 2 Type II, their pen test results, and their incident history. How do they handle our members' data? Where is it stored? How long is it retained? Can we audit?
Data Transmission to IDV: What data goes to the IDV provider? Can we minimize it? Do we need to send full SSN, or can we send last four for verification? Are we sending images of documents? Where do those images live?
Response Handling: What comes back from the IDV provider? Are we logging their response? If they return risk scores or fraud indicators, that's valuable but sensitive. Make sure it's protected appropriately.
Fraud Vectors: Synthetic identity fraud is the big risk here. How do we detect synthetic identities? Is the IDV provider doing document verification, biometric matching, database checks? What's our fallback if IDV is inconclusive?
Session Management: This is a multi-step flow. How are we maintaining session state? JWTs? Server-side sessions? What's the session timeout? Can an attacker hijack a partially completed application?
Rate Limiting & Bot Protection: Account opening is a target for automated attacks. Are we rate limiting by IP? Device fingerprinting? CAPTCHA? We need to make automated abuse expensive.
Error Handling: When IDV fails, what do we show the user? Don't leak information about why verification failed—that helps fraudsters tune their approach. Generic 'verification unsuccessful' is safer.
Audit Logging: Every account opening attempt needs to be logged with sufficient detail for fraud investigation and regulatory examination. But don't log PII in plaintext—tokenize or encrypt sensitive fields.
Fallback Process: What happens when the IDV provider is down? Do we queue applications? Fail open? Fail closed? This needs to be a deliberate decision with business input.
I want to see the architecture diagram, the data flow, and the threat model before we write code. Let's get security right by design, not by patching later."

Scenario 5: Strategic Technology Discussion
You: "Marc, looking 3-5 years out, what technology trends should credit unions be preparing for?"
Marc: "I see several trends that will reshape how we build and operate technology:
Composable Architecture & Best-of-Breed: The monolithic platform era is ending. The future is composable—best-of-breed components assembled via APIs. This means every credit union needs to become an integration shop. We need API management, integration patterns, and vendor management as core competencies. The winners will be those who can assemble the best components quickly, not those locked into a single vendor's roadmap.
AI as Infrastructure: AI is moving from science project to infrastructure. In 3-5 years, every customer interaction, every risk decision, every operational process will have AI augmentation. The question isn't whether to use AI—it's whether you have the platform to deploy it reliably. That means investing now in data infrastructure, MLOps, and governance frameworks.
Real-Time Everything: Members expect real-time. Real-time payments (FedNow, RTP), real-time fraud decisions, real-time balance updates, real-time notifications. Batch processing is becoming a legacy concept. This requires architectural investment—event-driven systems, streaming infrastructure, and modernized core banking.
Zero-Trust Security: The perimeter is dead. With cloud, remote work, and API-driven integrations, we can't rely on network boundaries for security. Zero-trust—verify every request, assume breach, least privilege—becomes mandatory. This is a multi-year transformation of identity, access management, and network architecture.
Embedded Finance: Banking services will increasingly be embedded in non-banking contexts. Members may not come to our app—they'll access banking where they already are. This means our services need to be API-accessible and embeddable. We need to think about our capabilities as products that can be consumed by partners, not just members directly.
Talent Model Evolution: The technology talent model is changing. We can't compete with big tech on compensation, so we need to compete on mission, flexibility, and interesting problems. We also need to embrace AI-augmented development—tools that make our developers more productive. The credit unions that attract and retain technical talent will win.
The Consolidation Question: Technology costs are creating pressure for consolidation. Smaller credit unions can't afford modern technology stacks. I expect to see more shared services, CUSOs for technology, and mergers driven by technology economics. We need to decide: are we a technology leader that others could consolidate around, or are we at risk of being absorbed?
My advice: invest in three things now—data infrastructure, integration capabilities, and technical talent. Those are the foundations everything else depends on. Without those foundations, you'll be constantly playing catch-up."
